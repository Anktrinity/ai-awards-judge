Description of your technology
Our innovation is not a product in the traditional sense — it's a production methodology that fundamentally redeﬁnes 
the relationship between AI technology and human creativity in live event production.
The methodology centres on a deliberate separation of two functions that have always been inseparable in multi-
camera production: mechanical tracking (the repetitive pan, tilt, and zoom adjustments required to keep moving 
subjects in frame) and creative decision-making (composition, shot selection, visual storytelling, and editorial 
judgement).
Using ﬁve Sony BRC-AM7 AI PTZ cameras operated by just two crew members, we've demonstrated that AI can 
handle the mechanical tracking burden — which traditionally consumes approximately 80% of a camera operator's 
attention — while human operators focus exclusively on the creative dimensions of production that require human 
judgement, emotional intelligence, and storytelling instinct.
This creates a role that didn't previously exist in live event production: the multi-camera creative director. Instead of 
one operator managing one camera (performing both mechanical and creative functions), a single operator now 
creatively directs two or three AI-assisted cameras simultaneously — making compositional decisions, managing shot 
variety, and directing the visual narrative across multiple viewpoints.
The practical impact is measurable. At Goldwell's Celebration of Color — a high-pressure runway show at Exhibition 
White City, London — our two-person crew delivered broadcast-grade ﬁve-camera coverage that would traditionally 
require ﬁve operators. The AI operated at approximately 90% autonomy during the live show, and the quality was 
independently veriﬁed by Vibration Production, the AV supplier managing the event.
Critically, this methodology is distinct from both automated lecture capture (where cameras operate entirely without 
human creative input) and traditional PTZ remote operation (where an operator still manually controls each camera's 
tracking). Our approach occupies a new space between these: AI handles the mechanics, humans handle the 
creativity, and the result is production quality that exceeds either approach alone.
The methodology proved so compelling that Sony Professional Europe commissioned gassProductions to produce the 
oﬃcial global case study ﬁlm, now distributed worldwide. Our operational feedback also directly inﬂuenced four 
ﬁrmware improvements in Sony's V3.0 update announced at ISE 2026, demonstrating that this methodology doesn't 
just use AI technology — it actively advances it.
The approach is inherently scalable and replicable. Any production company with BRC-AM7 cameras can adopt the 
same methodology. Our case study provides a documented blueprint, and the ﬁrmware improvements we inﬂuenced 
are available to all operators globally. This is now gassProductions' standard operating model for corporate events.

Explain how the AI works in your solution?
The Sony BRC-AM7 uses on-device AI powered by Sony's BIONZ XR processor — the same chip found in their 
ﬂagship cinema and mirrorless cameras. All AI inference runs locally on the camera hardware with zero cloud 
dependency, zero data transmission, and zero latency.
The AI engine performs real-time skeletal tracking, head detection, face recognition (with priority subject registration), 
and clothing pattern analysis to maintain consistent framing of moving subjects. It can simultaneously track up to 8 
individuals across more than 150 composition presets, automatically adjusting pan, tilt, and zoom to keep subjects 
framed according to operator-deﬁned parameters.
The camera features a 1.0-inch Exmor RS CMOS sensor with 20x optical zoom (24–480mm equivalent) and up to 40x 
Clear Image Zoom in Full HD — delivering broadcast-quality imagery that matches or exceeds traditional manned 
camera positions.
Critically, the AI handles the mechanical burden of tracking — the repetitive pan/tilt/zoom adjustments that traditionally 
consume approximately 80% of a camera operator's attention. Manual operator override is available at any time, 
allowing instant human intervention for creative decisions or unexpected scenarios. This is not fully autonomous ﬁlming 
— it's AI-assisted production where the technology handles mechanics and humans retain complete creative control.
What speciﬁc problem does this AI solve?
In traditional multi-camera live production, every camera operator performs two fundamentally diﬀerent tasks 
simultaneously: mechanical tracking (keeping subjects framed through constant pan, tilt, and zoom adjustments) and 
creative decision-making (composition, shot selection, storytelling). These tasks require diﬀerent skills — one is 
repetitive and reactive, the other is creative and intentional — but they've always been coupled in a single role.
This coupling creates three problems. First, it's expensive: ﬁve cameras means ﬁve operators, making broadcast-
quality multi-camera production economically inaccessible for most events. Second, it's limiting: because operators 
spend approximately 80% of their attention on mechanical tracking, only 20% goes toward creative decisions — 
limiting the creative potential of multi-camera production. Third, it's space-intensive: ﬁve manned camera positions 
require dedicated ﬂoor space.
Our methodology solves all three problems by deliberately decoupling these functions. AI handles the mechanical 
tracking entirely, freeing operators to focus 100% of their creative attention across multiple cameras. The result is 60% 
fewer crew, reduced physical footprint, and more creative, more dynamic coverage because operators are no longer 
cognitively overloaded with mechanical tasks.
This makes broadcast production accessible to events that previously couldn't aﬀord it, while simultaneously 
improving creative quality.

MODEL TRANSPARENCY (optional)
What type of AI model or technique does your solution use? Please provide a simple, high-level explanation that
helps judges understand the approach (no conﬁdential or proprietary details required).
The AI system used in our production methodology is entirely contained within the Sony BRC-AM7 PTZ camera 
hardware. There is no external AI platform, cloud processing, or third-party machine learning service involved.
The core AI engine runs on Sony's BIONZ XR processor — the same chip used in Sony's ﬂagship Alpha mirrorless 
cameras. This processor handles all inference tasks locally, ensuring zero-latency response and zero data transmission 
to external servers.
The AI capabilities include four primary functions. First, skeletal tracking: the system identiﬁes and follows human body 
structure, maintaining framing even when subjects turn away or are temporarily occluded. Second, head detection: a 
complementary tracking method for tightly packed groups or loose-ﬁtting clothing. Third, face recognition with priority 
subject registration: operators can register individuals as priority subjects, ensuring preferential framing on key 
speakers, performers, or VIPs. Fourth, clothing pattern analysis: the system identiﬁes subjects by distinct patterns, 
particularly useful in fashion events where multiple subjects have similar characteristics.
The camera can simultaneously track up to 8 subjects and oﬀers more than 150 composition presets, giving operators 
granular control over AI framing. These presets deﬁne parameters such as headroom, lead room, subject size, and 
camera response speed.
Hardware speciﬁcations relevant to AI performance include: a 1.0-inch Exmor RS CMOS sensor (delivering superior 
image quality in challenging lighting), 20x optical zoom, up to 40x Clear Image Zoom in Full HD, and 4K/60p recording 
with simultaneous streaming capability.
Regarding data privacy: because all AI processing happens on-device, no personal data ever leaves the camera 
hardware. There is no cloud upload, no external data storage, and no transmission mechanism. When powered oﬀ, all 
tracking data is discarded. This makes the system GDPR-compliant for live events, as no personal data is processed 
beyond real-time, in-camera inference.
The AI operates as an assistive tool, not an autonomous system. At any point, a human operator can override the AI's 
decisions — adjusting framing, switching subjects, or taking full manual control. Our methodology explicitly preserves
