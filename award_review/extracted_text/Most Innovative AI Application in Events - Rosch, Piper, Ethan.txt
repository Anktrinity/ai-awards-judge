Description of your technology
Live events are made by people. The creative vision, the client relationships, the split-second decisions on a 
production ﬂoor. No technology replaces these. But the industry’s challenge has never been too many people — it’s 
too few people doing too much. Producers juggling 400 line items at midnight. Coordinators chasing quotes across 
twelve email threads. Finance teams catching a £40K overage on page seven of a PDF at 6pm on a Friday. That’s the 
job for AI. Not the creative seat — the work that buries the people sitting in it.
Event companies, production agencies and experiential marketing teams manage millions in costs across concurrent 
projects, many using spreadsheets or generalist tools like Monday.com (https://Monday.com). Event tools aren’t much 
better. ProCim feels designed for accountants. Scoro slows producers down. Showbiz Budgeting hasn’t evolved in 
decades. The result: budgets are clunky, manual documents disconnected from the work they’re tracking, and cash 
ﬂow is managed by gut feel and guesswork.
Before Piper, this kind of solution didn’t exist in events. Piper is an AI-native ﬁnancial platform purpose-built for event 
production, co-founded by two producers who’ve lived the problem and built on Anthropic’s Claude. The inspiration 
was personal: we were directly aﬀected when a 35-person London event agency collapsed under cash-ﬂow 
mismanagement, and Taylor, as a client, was sent a $10 million event budget by a 150-person agency that had pulled 
the wrong subtotals from its own spreadsheets. The industry needs more than better spreadsheets and nifty template 
oﬀerings. It needs a ﬁnancial backbone designed for how event businesses actually operate.
Event production isn’t static or repeatable. Change is the only constant. Vendor quotes arrive at midnight. Budgets are 
living documents that shift with every client conversation. Piper is built for that reality.

Questions / Answers
Explain how the AI works in your solution?
Four AI capabilities serve diﬀerent stages of the production ﬁnance workﬂow, all built on Anthropic's Claude.
Smart Start generates a structured budget scaﬀold from a natural language event description or uploaded ﬁles from 
previous events, including Excel and PDFs, creating sections and subsections in seconds from a blank page.
Smart Add lets producers build budget items through natural language or document upload. The AI generates 
structured line items with rates, quantities, section placement, and conﬁdence scores. Currency conversion is 
automatic. Producers iterate conversationally until the output is right.
AI Budget Review generates a categorised analytical report covering rate anomalies, duplicate charges, missing 
categories, and staﬃng gaps in under 60 seconds, constrained by strict JSON schema validation.
Ask Piper answers natural language questions about live budget data. "What happens if I add 100 guests?" generates 
cost projections, aﬀected line items, and a draft client communication.
Every prompt encodes production knowledge from decades of combined experience. The AI knows a scenic quote 
without transport is incomplete, that freelancer rates vary by role, and that a vendor summary page doesn't always 
match the detail.
No output reaches the budget without the producer's explicit approval. The AI works; the producer decides.
What speciﬁc problem does this AI solve?
Production ﬁnance is the operational backbone of every event company, and it's running on tools that weren't built for 
the job.
Event production budgets are high-stakes, living documents. A typical production has 200-500 line items across 15-30 
sections, with 20+ vendor quotes arriving in diﬀerent formats. The current workﬂow: read each PDF line by line, 
manually transcribe into a spreadsheet or legacy platform, assign sections, calculate markup, check your work, hope 
you didn't miss anything. A single AV quote takes an hour. A budget review is skipped when deadlines get tight. Cash-
ﬂow visibility is non-existent until it's too late.
The tools that exist, ProCim, Scoro, Showbiz Budgeting, were built for static, repeatable businesses. Event production 
is neither. Every show is diﬀerent, scope changes constantly, and the ﬁnancial picture shifts with every client 
conversation. These tools don't bend.
Piper solves this by applying AI to production ﬁnance from the architecture up, not as an add-on to an existing 
platform, but as the core interface. Budgets are built through conversation, vendor quotes are processed in seconds, 
reviews are generated systematically, and ﬁnancial questions are answered instantly. The entire workﬂow is reimagined 
for how event businesses actually operate.

Three things make Piper's approach genuinely new.
First: we are building for a vertical no one has built for. AI has been applied to attendee engagement, event marketing, 
matchmaking, registration, and content generation. Production ﬁnance, the system that determines whether a show 
makes or loses money, has been largely ignored. We haven't seen another AI-native platform built speciﬁcally for this 
function. Existing tools digitise spreadsheets. Piper replaces the workﬂow entirely.
Second: built by the people who live the problem. Adjacent-industry founders who don't understand events "fall out 
quite quickly because they don't get any traction," as one industry leader put it. Taylor and I have managed budgets on 
Apple product launches, NYFW, and Cannes Lions. Every feature exists because it was needed on a real show. That 
domain knowledge is encoded into the AI, so the platform understands event-speciﬁc patterns generic tools miss.
Third: complete ﬁnancial infrastructure, not a point solution. Piper isn't just AI-powered budgeting. It's a three-tier 
ﬁnancial platform: Piper Pitch (create and manage budgets), Piper Produce (track every cost from forecast through 
payment), Piper Finance (13-week cash-ﬂow visibility across all projects). No other platform we've found connects 
estimation to ﬁnancial outcome with AI running through every layer.
VALIDATION
How did you test, validate, or quality-check the AI outputs to ensure they were accurate, safe, or reliable?
Innovation demands trust. If producers don't trust the AI's output, they won't use it, and a ﬁnancial platform that isn't 
used is just a demo. We approached validation as a product decision, not a compliance exercise. The question wasn't 
"how do we prevent errors?" but "how do we build a system where the producer always has enough information to 
make a conﬁdent decision?"
Every AI output arrives with context for the producer's judgement. When Smart Add extracts a line item from a vendor 
quote, it includes a conﬁdence score: high when the rate and section placement are unambiguous, lower when the AI 
is interpreting unfamiliar formatting or ambiguous terminology. The producer sees exactly what the AI is certain about 
and what it's guessing. Low-conﬁdence items are visually ﬂagged. High-conﬁdence items still require explicit approval 
before they touch the budget.
The underlying architecture enforces this through strict output schemas. Smart Add can only return items with required 
ﬁelds: description, quantity, rate, unit type, section. AI Budget Review can only return ﬁndings with categories, severity 
levels, and speciﬁc line item references. If the AI produces anything that doesn't conform, the system surfaces an error 
rather than displaying partial or malformed results.
Prompts are versioned and managed through Langfuse, separate from application code. This means we iterate on AI 
quality at the speed of prompt engineering, deploying improvements in minutes rather than code release cycles. Smart 
Add has evolved through 8 major prompt versions, each tested against documents from real productions. When one 
version reduced accuracy on multi-page PDFs, we caught it through trace analysis within hours and rolled back 
without touching the codebase.
Isaac Kargar, PhD (Principal AI Scientist, published in IEEE) advises on model selection and validation architecture. Karl 
Johansson (CFO) ensures ﬁnancial workﬂows hold up against real-world production ﬁnance requirements. The result: 
innovation in what the AI can do, discipline in how it's allowed to do it.

CASE STUDY
Describe how the AI was used in practice.
To demonstrate Piper's capabilities, we built a real budget during a live call with a producer who manages her own 
budgets in spreadsheets. The scenario: a private members' club residency during London Book Fair at Old Sessions 
House, with a daytime exhibition for 80 guests, an evening party for 100, two hosted dinners, and production, AV, print, 
furniture, catering, and venue costs.
Before Piper, this process takes hours. A producer opens a blank template. Creates or repurposes section headers 
manually. Types in crew line items one by one, ﬁeld by ﬁeld, rate by rate. When vendor quotes arrive as PDFs, they 
read each page, transcribe pricing into cells, and manually assign categories. When the budget is done, they scan it 
themselves under deadline pressure, hoping they catch the errors. When the client asks "what changed?" they 
compare spreadsheet tabs and write an email from memory.
With Piper, it took minutes.
Smart Start generated a six-section budget scaﬀold from a natural language description of the event. No blank page, 
no manual setup.
Smart Add builds crew line items from a single sentence: "One exec producer at 650/day for seven days, one 
production coordinator at 450/day for ﬁve days, and three runners at 150/day for the show day." Five items generated 
with correct rates, quantities, and section placement.
For a quick reference, the producer wanted to use a quote from a previous show that was in US dollars. The producer 
uploaded it into Smart Add. The AI extracted every line item, including tables, chairs, soft furnishings, event spaces, 
and delivery charges, automatically converting USD to GBP and mapping items to the Furniture section. The producer 
removed two irrelevant charges (additional rental period, credit card service fee) and committed the rest.
Then the moment that deﬁnes what Piper can do that didn't exist before: a 13-page catering proposal. Beautifully 
designed, food photography across pages, pricing scattered throughout. The summary page showed £5,280. But the 
AI read every page and extracted itemised drink costs, cocktail stations, and beverage packages totalling over £2,000 
that the vendor's own summary had omitted. It ﬂagged three staﬃng positions with roles but no pricing. When we 
investigated why the initial pass missed some data, we discovered white-on-white text in the PDF. We re-engineered 
the prompt to search speciﬁcally for obscured text, and on subsequent passes the missing data was found. A human 
reviewing that document would never have seen it. A producer working from the summary page would have missed 
thousands in costs and unpriced labour.
The producer watching said: "It's actually ﬂagging mistakes ... I've not seen anything like this up to now."
AI Budget Review then analysed the growing budget and ﬂagged a markup discrepancy and questioned a few costs. 
Ask Piper modelled the impact of adding 100 guests, generating cost projections and a draft client email.
From blank page to reviewed, version-controlled, client-ready budget, with caught errors that would have cost 
thousands, in a fraction of the time.

What type of AI model or technique does your solution use? Please provide a simple, high-level explanation that
helps judges understand the approach (no conﬁdential or proprietary details required).
We built Piper on Anthropic's Claude, and the choice was as much philosophical as technical.
After testing multiple foundation models, Claude stood out for two reasons. First, its structured output capabilities: 
Piper needs the AI to return precisely formatted ﬁnancial data, not freeform text. Claude consistently produces output 
that conforms to strict schemas, including item descriptions, quantities, rates, section placements, and conﬁdence 
scores, without hallucinating ﬁelds or breaking structure. Second, Anthropic's approach to AI safety: for a platform 
handling real production budgets where a misextracted rate directly costs money, we wanted a model built by a 
company that thinks carefully about autonomy and constraint.
The AI runs at two speeds. Complex tasks like extracting 40 line items from a multi-page vendor PDF, analysing a 
complete budget for anomalies, or modelling the ﬁnancial impact of a scope change go through Claude Sonnet, which 
handles the deep context these operations require. Lighter tasks like quick help answers and simple workﬂow 
questions go through Claude Haiku for speed. The producer gets instant responses for simple queries and thorough 
analysis for complex ones, without having to choose.
What makes this more than a model integration is the domain layer. Every prompt carries event production knowledge 
from decades of combined experience. The AI knows that a scenic quote without transport is probably incomplete. It 
knows that freelancer day rates vary by role and seniority. It knows that a vendor summary page doesn't always match 
what's in the body of the document. It knows that an outdoor build without weather contingency is a risk that will 
surface on site. This domain encoding, event-speciﬁc operational knowledge rather than generic ﬁnancial intelligence, 
is what makes Piper's AI genuinely useful rather than generically capable.
Prompts live in Langfuse, completely separated from the application code. This separation matters because prompt 
quality determines output quality, and improving one shouldn't require shipping the other. We can reﬁne how the AI 
interprets a venue hire contract without deploying a new build. Every interaction is traced end-to-end, creating a quality 
system, not just a log.
No AI output touches budget data without explicit human approval.
The model proposes. The producer disposes.
That principle isn't a restriction on the AI. It's the reason producers trust it enough to change how they work.
All processing occurs via Anthropic's secure API, with row-level database security scoping every query to the 
authenticated user, JWT authentication, and no client-side secrets.
