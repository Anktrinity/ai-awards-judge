# Judge Calibration Memo

Purpose: keep scoring consistent, defensible, and fair across a mixed field of live products, embedded AI workflows, and earlier-stage entrants.

## 1. Portfolio-wide metrics vs entry-specific proof

### Rule
Reward portfolio-wide metrics as supporting evidence, but give the strongest Results scores only when the submission ties those metrics clearly to the specific entry, feature set, or case study being judged.

### Practical application
- **Strongest weighting:** entry-specific outcomes, named case studies, isolated before/after deltas, customer proof, repeatable deployment evidence.
- **Moderate weighting:** portfolio-wide performance totals that credibly show scale, maturity, or repeatability.
- **Lower weighting:** large aggregate numbers with weak attribution to the nominated product or collaboration.

### Scoring implication
- Portfolio numbers can support **Execution** and **Impact**.
- They should not automatically drive top-tier **Results** unless attribution is reasonably clear.

## 2. Early-stage originality without live results

### Rule
Do not penalize an entry simply because it is earlier-stage. Reward originality, product clarity, problem fit, and credible validation. But keep Results disciplined when live proof is limited.

### Practical application
An early-stage entry can still score very well if it shows:
- a genuinely differentiated approach
- strong workflow or product logic
- realistic validation using pilots, historical data, user testing, LOIs, or design partners
- a believable path to adoption and impact

### Scoring implication
- **Innovation** can still be high.
- **Execution** can still be solid to strong if the roadmap and implementation logic are credible.
- **Impact** can be moderate to strong if projected value is well grounded.
- **Results** should remain conservative without real-world outcome evidence.

## 3. Category fit vs overall excellence

### Rule
Score the quality of the entry, but give meaningful weight to how directly it fits the category brief.

### Practical application
If an entry is excellent but only adjacent to the category, do not ignore that mismatch.
Examples:
- integration automation may be very strong, but if the category is specifically registration and onboarding, the most direct attendee journey transformation should usually have an edge
- a collaboration entry should prove that the partnership model itself created distinctive value, not just that the vendor delivered well

### Scoring implication
- Category fit should most strongly affect **Innovation** and **Impact** interpretation.
- It can also influence tie-breaks when totals are close.

## 4. Evidence hierarchy

When deciding between close entries, prefer this order of evidence quality:
1. direct quantified outcomes tied to the nominated entry
2. named client or event case studies
3. repeat usage, retention, renewals, or partnerships
4. testimonials with specific operational detail
5. portfolio-level claims
6. aspirational or unverified claims

## 5. Tie-break rule

If entries are within about 2 to 5 points total, decide based on:
1. better evidence traceability
2. stronger category fit
3. clearer differentiation
4. stronger proof that AI is central, not cosmetic
5. more believable long-term value

## 6. Default scoring posture

- Be open to ambitious entrants.
- Be skeptical of vague metrics.
- Reward originality with discipline.
- Reward proof without over-favoring incumbents.
- Distinguish polished storytelling from real evidence.

## 7. Recommended house view for this judging set

For this batch, use the following stance:
- Treat portfolio-wide metrics as valid but secondary unless well tied to the specific entry.
- Be generous on **Innovation** for genuinely new approaches, even if commercial proof is still emerging.
- Be disciplined on **Results** when claims rely on missing dashboards, screenshots, or broad aggregate totals.
- In close calls, prefer the entrant whose evidence is easier to defend out loud in a judging discussion.
