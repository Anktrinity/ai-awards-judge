# Scoring Guide

## Core principle
Score each entry against the rubric first, then use category comparison to separate close contenders.

## Criteria

### Innovation
Ask:
- Is the approach genuinely original for the events space?
- Is AI central to the value, or just layered on top?
- Does it solve a real problem in a distinctive way?
- Is there evidence of thoughtful design rather than obvious deployment?

### Impact
Ask:
- What meaningful difference has this made, or is it credibly likely to make?
- Are there hard metrics, pilot data, adoption signals, or believable projections?
- Does the entry connect product capability to user or business outcomes?

### Execution
Ask:
- How strong is the implementation so far?
- Is the product or rollout coherent, professional, and credible?
- Did the team address adoption, workflow change, operational constraints, or roadmap clarity well?

### Results
Ask:
- What evidence shows outcomes already achieved?
- Are there testimonials, retention, client wins, repeat usage, conversions, partnerships, or recognition?
- For early-stage entries, is there meaningful traction and a believable path forward?

## Suggested score bands

### 90 to 100, Exceptional
- Distinctive, category-leading idea or execution
- Strong evidence, strong clarity, few weak points
- Likely finalist or winner in many fields

### 80 to 89, Strong
- Clear merit and convincing case
- Good evidence with some gaps or less differentiation
- Very plausible finalist range

### 70 to 79, Solid
- Competent and credible
- Some strengths, but either originality, proof, or polish is limited
- Worthy entry, not yet standout

### 60 to 69, Mixed
- Real promise, but notable gaps in evidence, clarity, or distinctiveness
- May be early, under-documented, or insufficiently substantiated

### Below 60, Weak
- Limited differentiation, thin evidence, or unclear execution
- Hard to defend as a leading submission based on materials provided

## Fairness rules for early-stage entries
- Do not punish an entrant solely for being pre-scale.
- Reward sharp problem definition, product coherence, credible pilots, user feedback, LOIs, partnerships, and realistic go-to-market thinking.
- Do not give top-tier Results scores without some form of evidence, even for strong concepts.

## Common red flags
- Generic AI language with little product detail
- Claims without baselines or attribution
- Large productivity promises unsupported by data
- Off-the-shelf feature wrapping presented as breakthrough innovation
- Strong concept but no proof of customer pull or operational viability

## Tie-break factors
When scores are close, prefer the entry that shows:
1. Better evidence traceability
2. Stronger category fit
3. Clearer differentiation
4. Stronger real-world adoption or customer proof
5. Better fit between problem, solution, and measurable outcome
6. More defensible long-term impact

## Calibration defaults
- Treat portfolio-wide metrics as supporting evidence unless they are tightly tied to the nominated entry.
- Be generous on Innovation for genuinely differentiated early-stage entries.
- Keep Results disciplined when live proof is thin or heavily dependent on missing dashboards, screenshots, or uploads.
- In close calls, prefer the entrant whose score is easier to defend with visible evidence.
